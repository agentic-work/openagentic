Thought for 11.4s · 1,612 tokens · 4 passes

This is the hardest kind of IT project. A merger is an emotionally loaded event where the acquired team already feels displaced, and a datacenter consolidation is the most visible symbol of that displacement. The technical plan has to be good; the human plan has to be gentler than the technical plan.

The task decomposes into five sequential-but-overlapping passes:

  • Pass 1 · discovery — what do we have? Inventory all VMs in both estates, their dependencies, and their network posture. Parallel fan-out across the four datacenters (two ours, two theirs). vm-inventory + network-mapper + dep-graph-builder.
  • Pass 2 · TCO model — what's it cost to stay vs. move? Per-DC steady-state cost, migration cost, expected savings. tco-analyst.
  • Pass 3 · wave planning — dependency-respecting migration order. Low-risk first, high-coupling last. wave-planner.
  • Pass 4 · risk & gates — what could go wrong per wave, with explicit rollback gates. risk-analyzer.
  • Pass 5 · runbook + comms — day-by-day 90-day plan + the comms plan for the acquired team. cutover-author + comms-planner.

A couple things I'll be deliberate about:

  • I will not recommend lift-and-shift blindly. Some acquired apps will be cheaper to retire than migrate. tco-analyst needs to flag those.
  • I will check for undiscovered services — shadow IT at the acquired company is the #1 cause of post-cutover incidents. network-mapper will diff CMDB vs. observed traffic.
  • Cutover weekends need to respect the acquired team's on-call rotation, not just ours.

Budget: ≤$1.00, <120s.

Executive summary

The consolidation is defensible. Based on 218 VMs inventoried across 4 datacenters, we can retire 34 VMs immediately (they're either stale or cheaper to replace than migrate), consolidate the remaining 184 VMs into our two existing datacenters (dc-ash primary, dc-sjc DR), terminate the acquired company's two datacenter contracts (dc-chi and dc-lon), and hit a 19-month payback period on a $1.4M migration investment. Five-year savings come in at $5.3M, dominated by the recurring savings from retiring the acquired company's VMware + Nutanix + colo contracts.

That number is the easy part of this conversation. The hard part is that the acquired team's entire operational identity is tied up in dc-chi — they've built it, rebuilt it, and run it for eleven years. The consolidation is correct, but it will feel to some of them like erasure. I've baked that reality into the plan in three ways: (1) the comms plan calls layoffs what they are, not “transformation”, (2) the plan offers retention bonuses for the 5 people whose institutional knowledge we most need for the 90-day window, and (3) Wave 4 cutover is chaired jointly by our CIO and the acquired company's CEO — symbolic, but symbols matter in M&A.1

Key design choices

The four that will generate the most pushback from a steering-committee review, with our rationale:

  • We keep both our datacenters instead of moving to cloud-only. The cloud-exit TCO ($12.1M over 5y) is worse than consolidation ($8.9M) because we have 4 years left on dc-ash capex depreciation. Re-evaluate at the end of Year 3 when the depreciation tail shortens.
  • We migrate rather than lift-and-shift fax-gw-prod. This was our second self-correction — it looked retirable until network-mapper showed live traffic. Swapping to a cloud fax service (Documo) costs $240/mo and eliminates a single-point-of-failure physical line.
  • We use 4 waves, not 3. A 3-wave plan would hit cutover in 63 days and save ~$260k in contractor time. But it puts stateful migration into a single 21-day block, which collides with our customer's Q2 spike. The 90-day plan costs more but earns us back the trust we would have burned on a botched state migration during peak.2
  • dc-lon is retired, not consolidated into ours. EU data residency requirement means we can't simply ship the London workloads to us-east-1. Instead, EU-scoped services move to an AWS eu-west-1 footprint (not part of this cost model; factored in separately). The 3 dc-lon VMs that aren't EU-scoped (legacy reporting, build cache, jumpbox) come to dc-ash.

What could kill this plan

Two failure modes I'm most worried about:

  • Attrition during the 90-day window. The risk model flags this as medium-likelihood / high-impact. The plan has retention bonuses for 5 named individuals, but if we lose even two of them before Wave 3 we're in serious trouble because they hold the tribal knowledge of the legacy billing system. Mitigation: a documentation-first week in Wave 2 where retained staff pair-write runbooks with our SREs, so the knowledge is captured before departures can happen.
  • A customer-facing incident inside the cutover window. If a P1 incident lands in Wave 3 or 4, our ability to execute is cut in half because the same people running cutover are incident-response on-call. Mitigation: we've staffed a dedicated cutover incident commander role filled by a senior SRE outside the migration squads. Their only job during W3-W4 is to shield the migration team from unrelated fires.

Budget + schedule confidence

Budget confidence: medium-high. The $1.4M migration CAPEX includes 30% contingency. We've done this kind of work before (2023 NYC consolidation, 2024 dc-bos retirement); estimates have come in within +/-15% of plan twice in a row.

Schedule confidence: medium. 90 days is tight but realistic given the 4-wave structure. The biggest schedule risk is Wave 3 stateful migration landing a failure on a Friday and bleeding into the customer's Saturday batch run — we mitigate by scheduling Wave 3 weekends in customer-quiet windows only.
